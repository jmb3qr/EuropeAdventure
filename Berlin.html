One of the guys had started a tech business in Chattanooga and was from Virginia. The bike tour was 6 hours long so you got an opportunity to talk to everyone.

Our first stop was the television tower which was built in the 70s and stands out so much in the city. The next place we went to was the national library which is right next to a University. During WWII students from the university raided the library and many other libraries around Berlin and took all of the books that were "anti Nazi," (almost every single book fit into this category) and they burned them. They built a memorial underneath the square that is a room with a bookshelf big enough to hold of the books that were burned. There is a little sunroof window on the ground that you can look through to see all of the book shelves.

Our next stop was Gendarmenmarkt which is a square containing two identical churches that were built facing each other and the concert hall. One of the churches was built for France and one for Germany and the two churches are almost identical except the German one was built a meter taller. On the corners of these churches there are statues and they are the only part of the church that are original. When Hitler found out that the SS was coming into Berlin he started hiding the cities art and valuables. Hitler loved the Prussian architecture so he wanted to preserve it. He went around taking down statues and then threw them in the rivers so that they would be safe. Years later all of these statues were pulled out of the river and placed on the corners of these churches.

Our next stop was Check Point Charlie. During the Berlin Wall there were three major check points (A, B, C) and Charlie was guarded by US soldiers. The part about this sight that made me mad was there were street guys dressed up in US uniforms and charging people money to take pictures with them in front of the gate. I thought that this was really disrespectful.

Our next stop was a piece of the Berlin Wall that sits right next to a building that was built before the war. This building was built by Nazis and was a very important office building. At the end of the war the Soviets took it over and made it offices for the most communist people. They needed to make sure that the people in this building were believers because it was right next to the wall. One day a man snuck his wife and two children into the building. They waited for nightfall and then they threw a rope from a window to the west side. They had people on the west side secure it and one by one they slide across in homemade canvas harnesses. It is recorded that the guard saw them escaping and decided not to shoot. On the top of the wall they originally put barbed wire but then they realized people were using the barbed wire to pull themselves over, so they replaces it with a piece of pipe that no one could grip on to.

We rode by one of the only guard towers that is still standing.

We rode through the Tier Garden which was one of the best parts of the day. The Garden is massive and smells so good and has such a cool breeze. We rode to a bear garden and enjoyed some sausages, schnitzel, and beers.

After lunch we rode past the Berlin Victory Column which is in place to celebrate the Prussian victory over Denmark, Austria, and France in three separate wars.

This Column is built in the middle of the Tier Garden and all around it you can see bullet holes in the concrete.

Our last stop was the Reichstag building which was home to the German Parliament from 1894 to 1933. The building was extremely damaged in WWII and after fell into disuse for the East Germans used a different building and so did the West Germans. When Germany was reunifies in 1990 they began to fully refurbish the buildings. Today the building has a large glass dome that people can go into to see a view of the city. This glass dome also has glass floors so the people can see down, it is supposed to be a symbol to the Parliament that the people are always on top and always have control.

It was really interesting to hear different people’s perspective. One interesting thing is that the cubes are painted with an anti-graffiti solution. The company that produced this solution is the same company that produced all of the gas for the gas chambers during the war. When this was discovered the community was outraged and in order to make amends the company donated all of the solution. We talked about how the cubes make you feel lost but at the same time give you a sense of hope because you can always see the way out. We also talked about how it was related to the Jewish cemetery in Prague.

They were so rude and then they ended up leaving the tour early and not tipping him.

Rob did a really good job of making all of the history make sense and filled in gaps that I had from the other tour. We learned the real reason the Berlin Wall came down. Gorbachev had started making some political reforms in the years prior which put the East Berlin officials in a bind. In order to manage they decided to make minor changes to their travel laws. On November 9th, 1989 East Germany had a meeting to discuss the new travel laws and a man Schabowski skipped out on the meeting. However, he was the man to announce this to the public later that evening. He went on national television and announced “trips abroad will be possible for every citizen.” Immediately the crowd freaked out and started asking “WHEN!?” To this he answered, “Right now immediately.” This started a massive dash for the wall and the border patrols had no idea what was happening. This picture painted on the East Side Gallery depicts the crowds rushing through the gates.

I left Bella and went on a communism tour lead by Rob. This tour was so good and I learned so much. Towards the end of WWII Roosevelt agreed to hold back and let The Soviet Union conquer Berlin which ended up being a huge mistake. This left The Soviet Union to think that they had more of a right to control Berlin. After the war The Allies realized that they did not see eye to eye with The Soviets so they separated Germany into West and East Germany and gave the Soviets East and Split the west between France, Britain, and the US. They did the same thing with Berlin, however, West Berlin laid in the middle of East Germany. In the begging of the division between the East and West was open and anyone could get a passport to go to the other. However, the conditions in East Germany were so bad that all of the educated people were leaving for the West. This caused a brain drain which made East Germany suffer even more. The East realized that everyone was leaving so they started to make secret plans to close the border between East and West Berlin. They kept all of the supplies outside of town and told everyone they were planning on building a large housing development. On June 15th, 1961 First Secretary of the Socialist Unity Party, Walter Ulbricht, stated in an international press conference, „ No one has the intention of erecting a wall,” and this was the first time the word “wall” was used. On the night of August 13th, 1961 The Soviets put up a barbed wire fence that completely surrounded West Germany (124 miles). From August 14th, 1961 to November 9th, 1989 only 5,000 people escaped most of these being in the first months of the temporary wall. A lot of the people that managed to escape were Soviet Soldiers. This famous picture is of a Soldier who decides to run to the East side while on post and is greeted by open arms on the West.

136 people died while attempting to cross over to West Berlin and there is a monument in the death strip for all of these people. The death strip was the space between the two walls. There was a wall on the East side and then another wall on the West side. Most people died in the death strip.

One of the most famous deaths is of a boy named Peter Fechter. Him and his friend were on lunch break in a building when they decided to make a run for it. A guard yelled at them to stop and Peter stopped while his friend kept running. His friend made it over the wall and the Guard shot Peter dead. He shot him as he was touching the western wall so the entire west heard his screams and could look over the wall and see his body. They were yelling at the guard to help him and the guard let Peter bleed to death in front of millions of Western eyes. This is one of the first big stories that allowed people to see what East Germany was doing to their people.

We heard a lot of escape stories from people windsurfing to Denmark to people building homemade hot air balloons. Another really famous story is of this old lady who is going out her window, being helped by people on the west, when a soviet troop starts to try and pull her back into the window.
